# Network measures

A network measure assigns a numerical score to each channel based on its position in the directed citation graph. Pulpit constructs edges from forwards and `t.me/` references: a directed edge from channel A to channel B means A regularly amplifies B's content, weighted by frequency relative to A's total output.

All measures can be used to size nodes in the graph viewer, making the most significant channels visually prominent.

## PageRank

*PageRank scores a channel by the importance of the channels that amplify it, not just by how many do.*

The score is the stationary distribution of a damped random walk on the citation graph: a channel inherits prestige from its forwarders, who inherit it from theirs, and a forward from a well-connected, influential channel counts for more than a forward from a marginal one. Pulpit builds edges in the citing→cited (amplifier→source) orientation that Brin & Page originally defined PageRank on, so the iteration `PR(v) = (1 − α)/N + α · Σᵤ PR(u) · w(u→v) / Σ_w w(u→w)` flows prestige toward sources by construction — no orientation tricks. The damping factor α defaults to the canonical 0.85; edge weights (fraction of the amplifier's own output dedicated to the source) row-normalise into transition probabilities, so the PageRank scoring is *weighted* and a single high-volume bond between two channels matters more than ten incidental mentions. The whole iteration is scale-invariant to the global edge-weight rescaling `build_graph` applies, so the rankings only depend on the *relative* tie strengths.

**References:**
- Brin, S. & Page, L. (1998) "The anatomy of a large-scale hypertextual web search engine." *Computer Networks and ISDN Systems* 30(1–7). [doi:10.1016/S0169-7552(98)00110-X](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0169-7552(98)00110-X)
- Page, L., Brin, S., Motwani, R. & Winograd, T. (1999) "The PageRank citation ranking: Bringing order to the Web." Stanford Tech. Rep. [http://ilpubs.stanford.edu:8090/422/](http://ilpubs.stanford.edu:8090/422/) — the canonical reference for PageRank applied to a *citation* network, which is exactly what Pulpit builds.

**Edge-weight choice.** PageRank rankings are **invariant** to `--edge-weight-strategy` `TOTAL` / `PARTIAL_MESSAGES` / `PARTIAL_REFERENCES`: NetworkX's `pagerank` row-normalises internally and the three differ only by a per-row constant, so they collapse to the same stochastic matrix. `NONE` is the only strategy that materially changes the PageRank ranking (it flattens each row to uniform out-distribution). See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies) for the full picture — the choice still affects HITS, betweenness/harmonic, Burt's constraint, communities, spreading efficiency, and trophic level.

**In practice:** a mid-sized channel consistently forwarded by the ten most connected outlets in a network will score higher than a large channel referenced only by peripheral accounts. PageRank identifies the channels that the network's own key players treat as authoritative — the sources that shape the agenda. It complements raw in-degree, which counts citations without discounting the cited-by-noise contribution from peripheral channels.

**Example.** In a network of nationalist Telegram channels, PageRank tends to surface the two or three outlets whose frames and narratives are picked up and redistributed by everyone else — the ideological anchors of the ecosystem, even if they don't have the largest subscriber counts.

## HITS Hub score

*A high hub score means this channel actively amplifies others — it is a distributor, not a producer.*

The HITS algorithm (Hyperlink-Induced Topic Search) of Kleinberg (1999) assigns two mutually reinforcing scores to every node — a **hub** score `h` and an **authority** score `a` — by jointly solving `a = Aᵀ h`, `h = A a`, the principal eigenvectors of `AᵀA` and `AAᵀ`. In Pulpit's amplifier→source orientation (the same citing→cited convention PageRank is built on), `A[u,v] = w(u→v)` is the tie strength from a citing channel `u` to a cited channel `v`, so a channel's hub score is the *weighted sum of the authority scores of the channels it forwards or references*: a node scores high as a hub only if its outgoing ties land on channels that are themselves recognised authorities. Pulpit runs the **weighted variant** (Kleinberg discusses weighting in §4 of the JACM paper; Borodin et al. 2005 surveys the formal extension) — edge weights enter the iteration directly, so a single dense forwarding relationship outweighs many incidental mentions. The implementation iterates the two updates with per-step max-rescaling for numerical stability and a final sum-to-1 normalisation, matching `nx.hits(normalized=True)`; Pulpit ships its own iteration so the same routine can be reused by the robustness attack module and so it degrades gracefully on the near-empty residual graphs that show up during attack simulations.

**References:**
- Kleinberg, J. (1999) "Authoritative sources in a hyperlinked environment." *Journal of the ACM* 46(5). [doi:10.1145/324133.324140](https://doi.org/10.1145/324133.324140) — the canonical reference; defines hub and authority on a directed link graph, exactly the structure of Pulpit's citation graph.
- Borodin, A., Roberts, G.O., Rosenthal, J.S. & Tsaparas, P. (2005) "Link analysis ranking: algorithms, theory, and experiments." *ACM Transactions on Internet Technology* 5(1). [doi:10.1145/1052934.1052942](https://doi.org/10.1145/1052934.1052942) — surveys the weighted extension Pulpit uses and its convergence behaviour.

**Edge-weight choice.** Unlike PageRank, HITS rankings *do* depend on the choice of `--edge-weight-strategy`: the iteration uses the raw weights (no row-normalisation), so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` produce materially different hub orderings. See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies).

**In practice:** hub score answers the question Pulpit's whole pipeline is built around — *who are the distributors?* It complements PageRank (which ranks the network's authoritative *targets*) and content originality (a behavioural signal from message content rather than link structure): high hub + low content originality = pure relay channel. Removing a top-ranked hub fragments information flow across the network without silencing any original source. Pair with [HITS Authority](#hits-authority-score) to read off the producer/distributor split as a single two-axis snapshot.

**Example.** In an Italian far-right Telegram ecosystem, a daily-digest aggregator with 8 000 subscribers forwards from twenty consistently-cited commentators. PageRank puts it in the middle of the pack because few channels cite *it back*; in-degree is low for the same reason. Its hub score, however, is the highest in the network: it links outward to the channels that the rest of the ecosystem treats as authorities, so the eigenvector iteration concentrates hub mass on it. Read alongside PageRank, the pair correctly diagnoses this channel as the network's main *redistribution layer* — a structurally critical node that classical prestige measures miss.

## HITS Authority score

*A high authority score means this channel is one of the original sources that the network's main distributors choose to spread.*

The counterpart to Hub from the same Kleinberg (1999) co-eigenvector system. Authority and hub are jointly defined by `a = Aᵀ h` and `h = A a` on the weighted adjacency `A[u,v] = w(u→v)`; iterating these updates converges `a` to the principal eigenvector of `AᵀA` (and `h` to that of `AAᵀ`). In Pulpit's amplifier→source orientation (the same citing→cited convention PageRank is built on), a channel's authority score is the *weighted sum of the hub scores of the channels that forward or mention it* — formally, of its predecessors in the citation graph — so a node ranks high as an authority only when it is cited by channels that are themselves recognised distributors. Prestige carries provenance: *who* you are cited by matters as much as how many cite you, and a citation from a top hub counts for more than one from a peripheral aggregator. Computation is shared with [HITS Hub](#hits-hub-score) — same weighted power iteration on the same `A` (Kleinberg discusses weighting in §4 of the JACM paper; Borodin et al. 2005 surveys the formal extension), same per-step max-rescaling for numerical stability, same final sum-to-1 normalisation matching `nx.hits(normalized=True)` — so the hub and authority columns on the channel table are always read off the same iteration.

**References:**
- Kleinberg, J. (1999) "Authoritative sources in a hyperlinked environment." *Journal of the ACM* 46(5). [doi:10.1145/324133.324140](https://doi.org/10.1145/324133.324140) — defines hub and authority on a directed link graph, exactly the structure of Pulpit's citation graph; gives the iterative formulation and the convergence-to-principal-eigenvector proof.
- Borodin, A., Roberts, G.O., Rosenthal, J.S. & Tsaparas, P. (2005) "Link analysis ranking: algorithms, theory, and experiments." *ACM Transactions on Internet Technology* 5(1). [doi:10.1145/1052934.1052942](https://doi.org/10.1145/1052934.1052942) — surveys the weighted extension Pulpit uses and the convergence behaviour of HITS variants relative to PageRank.
- Cha, M., Haddadi, H., Benevenuto, F. & Gummadi, K. P. (2010) "Measuring user influence in Twitter: the million follower fallacy." *ICWSM* 2010. [aaai.org/ojs/index.php/ICWSM/article/view/14033](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/ICWSM/article/view/14033) — the social-media-network argument for why hub-weighted indegree (≈ authority) is informative *beyond* raw follower count and degree; Pulpit's [authority-weighted reach D](interesting-messages.md#authority-weighted-reach-d) uses HITS authority as its fallback weight on exactly this basis.

**Edge-weight choice.** Like Hub and unlike PageRank, HITS Authority rankings *do* depend on `--edge-weight-strategy`: the iteration consumes the raw weights without row-normalisation, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` produce materially different authority orderings. See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies).

**In practice:** authority is the prestige-side answer to *who is the source?* It complements PageRank — both reward being cited by central channels, but HITS authority specifically rewards being cited by good *hubs*, while PageRank rewards being cited by anyone with high PageRank (a node need not be a hub at all to vote). When authority and PageRank diverge sharply on a node, the divergence is itself informative: a channel with very high authority but middling PageRank is a niche source the broader network reaches only through the distribution layer; the opposite pattern signals a source cited broadly but rarely by the network's main relays. The same score is reused in two other places in Pulpit, and pulling those into a single analysis is usually how authority earns its keep: as the per-message [authority-weighted reach D](interesting-messages.md#authority-weighted-reach-d) when PageRank is not in `--measures`, and as a [robustness attack strategy](robustness-analysis.md#attack-strategies) that removes the network's primary sources first — typically the most informative *prestige* attack on Telegram political networks, because authorities are usually the moderation target rather than aggregators. Pair with [HITS Hub](#hits-hub-score) for the canonical producer/distributor two-axis snapshot.

**Example.** A political strategist's channel with 5 000 subscribers ranks as the top authority in a network because fifteen high-traffic aggregator channels — all of them top-ten hubs — forward its posts daily. Subscriber count would mislead an analyst into ignoring it; PageRank would rank it high but not first; HITS authority puts it at the top because the channels that cite it are precisely those whose hub scores are highest. Read together with the daily-digest aggregator from the Hub example above, this is the canonical producer/distributor pair: low authority + high hub on one side, high authority + low hub on the other.

## Betweenness centrality

*A high betweenness score means this channel sits on many of the shortest paths connecting other channels — it is a broker.*

Betweenness centrality (Freeman 1977) scores each channel by the fraction of all directed shortest paths that route through it: `C_B(v) = Σ_{s ≠ v ≠ t} σ_st(v) / σ_st`, where `σ_st` is the number of shortest paths from `s` to `t` and `σ_st(v)` the number that pass through `v`. Pulpit computes it on the directed citation graph (paths follow the amplifier→source orientation `build_graph` writes) via Brandes' (2001) `O(NM + N² log N)` weighted algorithm, the NetworkX implementation behind `nx.betweenness_centrality`. Edge weights enter as **distances**, with tie strength inverted to proximity (`distance = 1 / weight`, Opsahl, Agneessens & Skvoretz 2010): every shortest-path routine minimises the distance attribute, so passing strength straight through would route paths *around* the strongest ties — the lightly-trafficked channel would score as the broker. With `1/weight`, heavily-forwarded edges become *short*, paths preferentially follow real flow, and the betweenness measure, the harmonic centrality measure, and the brokerage robustness attack all agree on what "close" means. NetworkX's default normalisation is left on, so scores are divided by `(n−1)(n−2)` for directed graphs and stay in `[0, 1]`.

**References:**
- Freeman, L.C. (1977) "A set of measures of centrality based on betweenness." *Sociometry* 40(1). [doi:10.2307/3033543](https://doi.org/10.2307/3033543) — the canonical reference; defines betweenness as the shortest-path traversal count and gives the broker interpretation Pulpit relies on.
- Brandes, U. (2001) "A faster algorithm for betweenness centrality." *Journal of Mathematical Sociology* 25(2). [doi:10.1080/0022250X.2001.9990249](https://doi.org/10.1080/0022250X.2001.9990249) — the practical algorithm Pulpit uses via NetworkX, replacing the naive `O(N³)` accumulation with the now-standard shortest-path-dependency back-propagation.
- Opsahl, T., Agneessens, F. & Skvoretz, J. (2010) "Node centrality in weighted networks: Generalizing degree and shortest paths." *Social Networks* 32(3). [doi:10.1016/j.socnet.2010.03.006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.socnet.2010.03.006) — the standard treatment of the strength→proximity (`1/weight`) inversion shared with harmonic centrality.

**Edge-weight choice.** Like HITS and unlike PageRank, betweenness rankings depend on `--edge-weight-strategy`: the `1/weight` distance is consumed directly, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` (which collapses paths to plain hop counts) produce materially different orderings. See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies).

**In practice:** betweenness is Pulpit's primary measure for cross-community dynamics. A channel that bridges two ideological camps will score high even without having high prestige within either camp — and prestige measures (PageRank, HITS) routinely miss such brokers because they may be cited by few others and themselves cite many. The same computation feeds three derived constructs that the orchestrator shares across calls: **Bridging Centrality** (Hwang et al. 2008, `BRIDGINGCENTRALITY`) up-weights brokers wedged between *high-degree* regions; **Community Bridging** (`BRIDGING`) up-weights brokers spanning *detected communities*; and the [**brokerage robustness attack**](robustness-analysis.md#brokerage-attack-betweenness) ranks channels by removal-time fragmentation impact, typically the most destructive class of attack on Telegram political networks because such ecosystems are often held together by a small number of bridge channels.

**Example.** A channel that regularly references both a cluster of religious nationalist outlets and a cluster of economic libertarian outlets — groups that don't directly cross-reference — appears as the bridge between two otherwise separate ecosystems. Its in-degree is modest and its PageRank ordinary, because neither cluster cites it heavily; yet every shortest path between the two ecosystems runs through it, so its betweenness is among the highest in the network. It is the main vector through which narratives migrate from one community to the other, and precisely the kind of channel whose removal under the [betweenness robustness attack](robustness-analysis.md#brokerage-attack-betweenness) would fragment the coalition.

## In-degree centrality

*The simplest prestige measure: what fraction of the network's other channels cite this one?*

In-degree centrality is the canonical degree centrality of a directed graph, normalised for cross-network comparability: `C_in(v) = deg_in(v) / (n − 1)`, where `deg_in(v)` is the number of *distinct* predecessors of `v` in the citation graph (channels that forward from or reference `v`) and `n − 1` is the maximum achievable — the score the centre of a star graph would reach. Freeman (1978) introduced the `(n − 1)` normalisation precisely so degree scores live on a `[0, 1]` scale independent of network size; Wasserman & Faust (1994, §5) place in-degree and out-degree on the *prestige* and *expansiveness* axes of a directed graph, the framing Pulpit adopts. Pulpit's edges are oriented amplifier→source (citing→cited), so a channel's in-degree counts how many *distinct* channels treat it as a source — independent of how often each does so.

The implementation calls `nx.in_degree_centrality`, which is **unweighted by design**: every predecessor counts once, regardless of how many forwards or references that predecessor sent. The weighted counterpart, the in-strength `Σ_u w(u→v)` (column **In-strength** in the channel table, key `in_deg`), is reported separately by `apply_base_node_measures` and aggregates tie *intensity* rather than tie *count* — so a channel cited by ten outlets once each has the same in-degree centrality as one cited by the same ten outlets a hundred times each (the in-strength differs by 100×). The two are not redundant. Because the unweighted, normalised in-degree hits exactly `1.0` on a star graph's hub — the textbook theoretical maximum — Freeman's centralisation formula is well-defined for it, and it is one of the measures the [CENTRALIZATION group](whole-network-statistics.md#freeman-centralisation-per-measure) reports; in-strength is deliberately excluded, since strength has no star-based maximum and the generic Freeman normaliser would be too loose to be informative.

**References:**
- Freeman, L.C. (1978) "Centrality in social networks: Conceptual clarification." *Social Networks* 1(3). [doi:10.1016/0378-8733(78)90021-7](https://doi.org/10.1016/0378-8733(78)90021-7) — the canonical reference; defines degree, betweenness and closeness centrality and the `(n − 1)` normalisation that places degree scores on `[0, 1]`.
- Wasserman, S. & Faust, K. (1994) *Social Network Analysis: Methods and Applications*. Cambridge University Press. [doi:10.1017/CBO9780511815478](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815478) — chapter 5: the textbook treatment of in-degree and out-degree as the *prestige* and *expansiveness* components on a directed graph, the reading Pulpit follows.

**Edge-weight choice.** In-degree centrality is **invariant** to `--edge-weight-strategy`: NetworkX's implementation discards edge weights and counts distinct predecessors, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` all produce identical orderings. Use the **In-strength** column, PageRank or HITS Authority for a weighted prestige measure. See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies).

**In practice:** in-degree centrality answers the most direct question Pulpit's pipeline can ask of the citation graph — *which channels are the most-cited sources?* It is the only purely descriptive prestige measure (no random walks, no eigenvector iteration, no shortest paths), which makes it the appropriate baseline to read alongside PageRank and HITS Authority. The informative cases are the disagreements: a channel high on in-degree but middling on PageRank or authority is *broadly* cited — often by peripheral accounts whose own prestige is low — whereas a channel high on PageRank or authority but middling on in-degree is cited *selectively* by the network's main hubs. Reading the two together separates "popular" from "prestigious".

**Example.** The official channel of a political party often tops the in-degree ranking — it is a routine reference point for many small accounts across the network. Its PageRank may be only middling, however, because most of those citations come from peripheral channels that themselves carry little prestige. A second channel — say, a strategist's commentary feed with a fraction of the in-degree — may sit much higher on PageRank and HITS Authority because the few channels that cite it are precisely the network's main hubs. In-degree alone correctly identifies the party channel as the most-cited reference point; only the comparison with PageRank reveals which of the two is *strategically* central.

## Out-degree centrality

*The simplest expansiveness measure: what fraction of the network's other channels does this one cite?*

Out-degree centrality is the directed counterpart of [in-degree centrality](#in-degree-centrality): `C_out(v) = deg_out(v) / (n − 1)`, where `deg_out(v)` is the number of *distinct* successors of `v` in the citation graph (channels that `v` forwards from or references) and `n − 1` is the maximum achievable — the score reached by the centre of an out-star. Freeman (1978) introduced the `(n − 1)` normalisation precisely so degree scores live on a `[0, 1]` scale independent of network size; Wasserman & Faust (1994, §5) place in-degree and out-degree on the *prestige* and *expansiveness* axes of a directed graph, and Pulpit's measure pair maps directly onto that framing. Pulpit's edges are oriented amplifier→source (citing→cited), so a channel's out-degree counts how many *distinct* channels it treats as a source — independent of how many forwards or references it sends each of them.

The implementation calls `nx.out_degree_centrality`, which is **unweighted by design**: every successor counts once, regardless of how many forwards or references the edge carries. The weighted counterpart, the out-strength `Σ_w w(v→w)` (column **Out-strength** in the channel table, key `out_deg`), is reported separately by `apply_base_node_measures` and aggregates tie *intensity* rather than tie *count* — so a channel forwarding once each from ten sources has the same out-degree centrality as one forwarding a hundred times from the same ten sources (the out-strength differs by 100×). The two are not redundant. Because the unweighted, normalised out-degree hits exactly `1.0` on an out-star's hub — the textbook theoretical maximum — Freeman's centralisation formula is well-defined for it, and it is one of the measures the [CENTRALIZATION group](whole-network-statistics.md#freeman-centralisation-per-measure) reports; out-strength is deliberately excluded, since strength has no star-based maximum and the generic Freeman normaliser would be too loose to be informative.

**References:**
- Freeman, L.C. (1978) "Centrality in social networks: Conceptual clarification." *Social Networks* 1(3). [doi:10.1016/0378-8733(78)90021-7](https://doi.org/10.1016/0378-8733(78)90021-7) — the canonical reference; defines degree, betweenness and closeness centrality and the `(n − 1)` normalisation that places degree scores on `[0, 1]`.
- Wasserman, S. & Faust, K. (1994) *Social Network Analysis: Methods and Applications*. Cambridge University Press. [doi:10.1017/CBO9780511815478](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815478) — chapter 5: the textbook treatment of in-degree and out-degree as the *prestige* and *expansiveness* components on a directed graph, the reading Pulpit follows.

**Edge-weight choice.** Out-degree centrality is **invariant** to `--edge-weight-strategy`: NetworkX's implementation discards edge weights and counts distinct successors, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` all produce identical orderings. Use the **Out-strength** column for a weighted measure of citing *intensity*; there is no weighted "expansiveness" eigenvector analogue (HITS Hub is the closest, but it propagates through the network rather than counting direct ties). See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies).

**In practice:** out-degree centrality answers the dual question to in-degree — *which channels cast the widest net?* It is the only purely descriptive expansiveness measure (no random walks, no eigenvector iteration, no shortest paths), which makes it the appropriate baseline to read alongside HITS Hub. The informative cases are the disagreements: a channel high on out-degree but middling on HITS Hub cites *broadly* but mostly low-prestige sources, whereas a channel high on HITS Hub but middling on out-degree cites *selectively* but its sources happen to be the network's main authorities. Pairing in-degree with out-degree completes the two-axis classification Wasserman & Faust (1994 §5) lay out: pure producers (high in, low out), pure distributors (high out, low in), true hubs (high on both), and peripheral leaves (low on both).

**Example.** A daily-digest aggregator that links to thirty distinct sources each day routinely tops the out-degree ranking — its out-edges fan across a large fraction of the network. Its in-degree is often low (few channels cite a curator back), so the in/out pair clearly labels it as a *distributor*. A second channel — say, an opinion writer with a fraction of the out-degree — forwards from only five sources, but those five are the network's most-cited authorities, so its HITS Hub score is much higher. Out-degree correctly identifies the digest as the network's broadest amplifier; only the comparison with HITS Hub reveals that the opinion writer's *narrower* citation pattern is the more strategically targeted one.

## Harmonic centrality

*A high harmonic centrality score means many other channels can reach this one through short, strongly-tied citation chains — a generalisation of in-degree from direct citers to all citers via multi-hop paths.*

Harmonic centrality (Marchiori & Latora 2000; Rochat 2009; Boldi & Vigna 2014) scores each channel by the sum of reciprocal shortest-path *distances from every other channel to it*: `C_H(u) = Σ_{v ≠ u} 1/d(v, u)`, normalised to the mean reciprocal distance `C_H(u) / (n − 1)`. The reciprocal-distance form has a crucial property the classical closeness measure lacks: unreachable pairs contribute zero rather than infinity, so the measure stays well-defined on graphs that are not strongly connected — exactly the sparse, partially disconnected citation graphs Pulpit builds. Boldi & Vigna (2014) give the axiomatic case for using harmonic over closeness in this setting.

Pulpit computes it via `nx.harmonic_centrality(g, distance="distance")` on the directed citation graph in the as-built amplifier→source orientation — the same as-built convention shared with PageRank, HITS, betweenness and the bridging measures (CLAUDE.md spells out the convention; `SPREADING` and `TROPHICLEVEL` reverse the graph internally, and `BURTCONSTRAINT` symmetrises it). NetworkX's harmonic centrality sums distances *to* a node, not *from* it (see the function's [definition](https://networkx.org/documentation/stable/reference/algorithms/generated/networkx.algorithms.centrality.harmonic_centrality.html)), so on a citing→cited graph it measures **how easily the rest of the network reaches this channel** through citation chains — a closeness-style prestige index. It is the natural multi-hop generalisation of `INDEGCENTRALITY` (which counts only direct citers): a channel cited mostly *through* aggregators picks up harmonic mass that a 1-step in-degree misses entirely.

Edge weights enter as **distances**, with tie strength inverted to proximity (`distance = 1 / weight`, Opsahl, Agneessens & Skvoretz 2010), shared with [Betweenness](#betweenness-centrality) and the brokerage robustness attack — every shortest-path routine minimises the distance attribute, so passing strength straight through would route paths *around* the strongest ties. With `1/weight`, heavily-forwarded edges become *short*, and harmonic, betweenness, and the matching robustness scorers all agree on what "close" means. Because a weighted distance can fall below 1 (when an edge weight exceeds 1), the normalised score is **not bounded to [0, 1]** — read it relatively, not as a fraction.

[doi:10.1080/15427951.2013.865686](https://doi.org/10.1080/15427951.2013.865686) — the axiomatic case for harmonic over closeness on directed graphs that are not strongly connected (exactly Pulpit's setting), and the in-direction form `C_H(u) = Σ_v 1/d(v, u)` Pulpit computes.

**Edge-weight choice.** Like HITS and betweenness and unlike PageRank, harmonic-centrality rankings depend on `--edge-weight-strategy`: the `1/weight` distance is consumed directly, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` (which collapses paths to plain hop counts) produce materially different orderings. **In practice:** harmonic centrality answers a prestige-side question Pulpit's other measures don't quite get to — *which channels are deeply reachable from the rest of the network via short citation chains?* It complements three nearby measures and the informative cases are the disagreements:
- vs **In-degree**: in-degree counts only direct citers; harmonic also rewards being reached in two or three hops, so a channel cited mostly *through* a couple of aggregators picks up harmonic mass that in-degree misses.
- vs **PageRank**: PageRank rewards being cited by high-PageRank channels (eigenvector prestige); harmonic rewards being topologically *close* to many channels, regardless of how prestigious those citers are.
- vs **HITS Authority**: HITS authority rewards being cited by good *hubs*; harmonic does not care about its citers' roles, only their distance.

The matching [harmonic robustness attack](robustness-analysis.md#other-available-strategies) removes the most-reachable channels first — a reach-style prestige attack, complementary to the eigenvector-prestige attacks (PageRank, HITS authority) and the brokerage attack (betweenness). It is static-only because per-step recomputation would dominate runtime.

**Example.** A political strategist's channel `s` is directly cited by only three high-traffic aggregators, each of which is itself cited by twenty further channels in the same ecosystem. Its in-degree is 3 (modest), but its harmonic centrality is high — the three direct citers contribute `3 × 1 = 3` and the sixty two-hop citers contribute `60 × ½ = 30`, total 33 before normalisation. A second channel `b` is cited directly by twenty peripheral accounts that nothing else points to (so `b` and its citers form an isolated clump). Its in-degree is 20 (six times larger) and its harmonic sum is `20 × 1 = 20`, *lower* than `s`'s 33 — because `s`'s two-hop neighbourhood is six times wider than `b`'s direct one. In-degree ranks `b` higher; harmonic correctly identifies `s` as the structurally better-embedded source. Pulpit divides both by `n − 1` for the per-channel column; the ordering and the conceptual reading are unchanged.

## Burt's constraint

*A low Burt's constraint score means this channel sits at a structural hole — its contacts cluster into groups that do not interact, so it is the only bridge between them.*

Burt's constraint (Burt 1992, 2004) is the canonical structural-holes measure: it quantifies how much a channel's ego-network is *redundant* — how much of its interaction is invested in contacts that already cite each other directly or through shared intermediaries. The dyadic constraint of ego *v* on alter *w* is

> ℓ(v, w) = (p_vw + Σ_q p_vq · p_qw)²

where p_xy is x's share of total mutual interaction invested in y (`mutual_weight(x,y) / Σ_k mutual_weight(x,k)`) and the inner sum runs over v's other neighbours q. The first term is *direct* investment in w; the second sums *indirect* investment via shared neighbours q. Total constraint on v is `c(v) = Σ_w ℓ(v, w)`. A node whose contacts all cite each other has many large indirect terms and so a high constraint; a node whose contacts are mutually disjoint keeps only the direct terms and so a low constraint. Typical values fall in [0, 1]; the theoretical upper bound is ≈ 1.125, reached by perfectly redundant ego-networks (Burt 1992, ch. 2; Borgatti 1997). Lower is more broker-like — Burt's framing is that the *complement* of constraint is what gives brokers their information and control benefits.

Pulpit computes it via `nx.constraint(graph, weight="weight")`. **NetworkX symmetrises the directed citation graph internally**: the mutual weight of a pair (u, v) is `w(u→v) + w(v→u)`, and the neighbour set N(v) is `predecessors(v) ∪ successors(v)`. This matches Burt's original framing — structural holes are about *who ego's contacts are*, not which way the citation arrow points; a channel that forwards X and a channel forwarded by X are both contacts of v for the purpose of redundancy arithmetic. The score is therefore **invariant to edge direction** (unlike PageRank, HITS, betweenness, harmonic, bridging — the direction-sensitive measures), and `--edge-weight-strategy` enters only through the symmetrised mutual weight. Isolated nodes are undefined and exported as `null`.

**References:**
- Burt, R.S. (1992) *Structural Holes: The Social Structure of Competition*. Harvard University Press. [doi:10.4159/9780674038714](https://doi.org/10.4159/9780674038714) — canonical book-length introduction of constraint and the structural-holes framework; chapter 2 derives the dyadic and total constraint formulas and discusses the upper bound.
- Burt, R.S. (2004) "Structural holes and good ideas." *American Journal of Sociology* 110(2). [doi:10.1086/421787](https://doi.org/10.1086/421787) — the version of the formula NetworkX cites verbatim, applied to information access and innovation across organisational ego-networks. Also cited from Pulpit's [Vacancy analysis brokerage-overlap component](vacancy-analysis.md), so the same Burt-2004 reading anchors two distinct Pulpit features.
- Borgatti, S.P. (1997) "Structural holes: Unpacking Burt's redundancy measures." *Connections* 20(1). A careful dissection of what constraint actually measures, including the dense-ego-network regime where values can slightly exceed 1 and the cases where constraint diverges from intuitive structural-hole readings.

**Edge-weight choice.** Like HITS and the betweenness/harmonic family, Burt's constraint rankings depend on `--edge-weight-strategy`: the mutual weights `w(u→v) + w(v→u)` enter the row normalisation, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` (binary ties) produce materially different orderings. See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies). Direction, however, does *not* affect it — see the paragraph above.

**In practice:** Burt's constraint is Pulpit's answer to a specific brokerage question that betweenness can miss. Betweenness rewards lying on many *shortest* paths between far-apart parts of the network — a global brokerage signal that floods toward high-traffic hubs. A small channel that connects two otherwise-disconnected, low-traffic clusters can have negligible betweenness (few shortest paths reach those clusters at all) yet very low constraint — its ego-network has no triangles, so the indirect terms in `c(v)` stay near zero. Pulpit's matching [robustness attack](robustness-analysis.md#other-available-strategies) removes low-constraint nodes first (inverse ranking) and identifies the quiet brokers whose loss would isolate small but distinct sub-communities. Pair Burt's constraint with neighbouring measures to disambiguate broker types:
- vs **`BETWEENNESS`**: low constraint + low betweenness = peripheral broker connecting *small* groups; low constraint + high betweenness = the global flow broker (and global ego-network broker simultaneously).
- vs **`LOCALCLUSTERING`**: low constraint + low clustering = a broker that sits *between* groups; high constraint + high clustering = a member of a tightly woven echo chamber.
- vs **`BRIDGINGCENTRALITY`** (Hwang et al. 2008): Hwang's measure is a degree-based topological-bridge index; Burt's is an interaction-redundancy index. Both should agree on the canonical low-degree-broker case; disagreement points to brokers that interact heavily with their few contacts (low Burt) but whose contacts are not high-degree hubs (low Hwang) — local brokers rather than network-wide ones.

**Example.** In a mapped Italian political network, a channel with modest PageRank and a few thousand subscribers has a Burt's constraint of 0.08 — the lowest in the network. Investigation reveals it is run by a political operative who curates content from both far-right and religious-nationalist ecosystems, forwarding selectively to each. Its in-degree is unremarkable and its betweenness is mid-pack (the two ecosystems it bridges are themselves small, so few shortest paths run through them), but its ego-network has almost no triangles: its contacts on each side do not cite each other, so the indirect terms in `c(v)` stay near zero and `c(v)` collapses to the direct terms alone. Burt's constraint is the only measure Pulpit computes that flags this channel as a structural broker — global-flow measures (PageRank, HITS, betweenness) and degree-based bridges (`BRIDGINGCENTRALITY`) all miss it.

## Local clustering

*A high local clustering score means this channel's immediate neighbourhood is closed in on itself — its citing/cited contacts also cite each other, forming tight mutual-amplification triangles.*

The directed local clustering coefficient (Fagiolo 2007) extends the Watts & Strogatz (1998) clustering coefficient to directed graphs by counting *all four* directed triangle patterns through a node — **cycle** (A→B→C→A), **middleman** (A→B, B→C, A→C), **in-triangle** (B→A, C→A, with a tie between B and C) and **out-triangle** (A→B, A→C, with a tie between B and C) — and dividing by the maximum number of such patterns the node's degree configuration could support. Concretely:

> c^D(u) = T^D(u) / [ 2 · ( d^tot(u) · (d^tot(u) − 1) − 2 · d^↔(u) ) ]

where `T^D(u)` is the total count of directed triangles through `u` summed over all eight pattern orientations, `d^tot(u) = d^in(u) + d^out(u)` is the total degree, and `d^↔(u)` is the count of reciprocal (bilateral) edges incident to `u`. The score lies in `[0, 1]` — 1.0 when every pair of `u`'s contacts is connected in some direction (the locally complete case) and 0.0 when no directed triangle passes through `u`. The 0.0 fallback also catches isolated nodes and nodes with `d^tot(u) < 2`, where no triangle is geometrically possible.

Pulpit computes it via `nx.clustering(graph)` on the directed citation graph **without a `weight=` argument**, so the unweighted Fagiolo form is used — each citation counts as 1 in the triangle tally regardless of forwarding intensity. The formula is **invariant under graph reversal**: reversing every edge permutes pattern types (in-triangle ↔ out-triangle, cycle ↔ cycle, middleman ↔ middleman) but leaves `T^D(u)`, `d^tot(u)` and `d^↔(u)` unchanged. The score therefore behaves like Burt's constraint in this respect — neither `--edge-weight-strategy` nor citation direction affects the ranking, even though every other directed-triangle-aware measure in the suite (PageRank, HITS, betweenness, harmonic, bridging) does respond to one or both.

**References:**
- Fagiolo, G. (2007) "Clustering in complex directed networks." *Physical Review E* 76(2), 026107. [doi:10.1103/PhysRevE.76.026107](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevE.76.026107) — the canonical extension of the clustering coefficient to directed graphs; defines the four directed triangle patterns and the "total" `C^D` Pulpit computes. NetworkX's `nx.clustering` cites this paper for the directed case.
- Watts, D.J. & Strogatz, S.H. (1998) "Collective dynamics of 'small-world' networks." *Nature* 393(6684). [doi:10.1038/30918](https://doi.org/10.1038/30918) — the original undirected local clustering coefficient; Fagiolo's directed extension reduces to this on symmetric graphs and inherits its echo-chamber / small-world interpretation.
- Saramäki, J., Kivelä, M., Onnela, J.-P., Kaski, K. & Kertész, J. (2007) "Generalizations of the clustering coefficient to weighted complex networks." *Physical Review E* 75, 027105. [doi:10.1103/PhysRevE.75.027105](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevE.75.027105) — surveys the weighted variants (geometric-mean / arithmetic-mean / maximum-edge), available in NetworkX as `nx.clustering(graph, weight=…)`. Pulpit currently uses the unweighted form; switching is a one-flag change to `apply_local_clustering` if a weighted interpretation is ever wanted.

**Edge-weight choice.** Unlike PageRank, HITS, betweenness, harmonic and Burt's constraint, Local Clustering is **invariant to `--edge-weight-strategy`**: `nx.clustering(graph)` is called without a `weight=` argument, so only the binary topology of citations enters the triangle count. Switching among `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` produces identical scores. The ranking is also invariant to citation direction (see paragraph above).

**In practice:** local clustering pinpoints channels whose *immediate ego-network is closed in on itself* — channels woven into tight triangle clusters rather than spread across mutually unrelated contacts. Pulpit's other measures characterise channels by their *role* (hub vs. authority, broker vs. embedded) or *behaviour* (originality, forwarding rate); local clustering instead measures the *density of interaction among a channel's contacts*, and the informative cases are the disagreements:

- vs **`BETWEENNESS`** / **`BURTCONSTRAINT`**: high betweenness or low constraint flag *brokers between* groups; high local clustering flags channels *inside* a closely-knit group. Low constraint + low clustering = a broker that sits between groups; high constraint + high clustering = a member of a tightly woven echo chamber — the canonical structural-holes vs. echo-chamber decomposition (Burt 1992 ch. 2). Pulpit's [Burt's constraint section](#burts-constraint) explicitly relies on this pair.
- vs **`CORENESS`**: K-core coreness rewards being *globally* embedded in the dense nucleus (the deepest k-shell on the symmetrised graph); local clustering rewards being *locally* embedded in triangles. A channel can sit in the top k-shell yet have low clustering (its core neighbours don't cite *each other*, only `u`), and vice versa — a tight little reciprocal triple far from the nucleus has high clustering but low coreness.
- vs **`HITSHUB`** / **`HITSAUTH`**: hub and authority scores track *directional* eigenvector roles (cited-by-hubs / cites-authorities). Local clustering is direction-invariant and asks only whether `u`'s contacts know each other — a hub that forwards from twenty unrelated sources scores low, a hub that forwards from twenty channels which all forward each other scores high.
- vs **`AMPLIFICATION`** / **`CONTENTORIGINALITY`**: those read off message attributes (forwarding volume, originality); local clustering is purely topological — high clustering can co-occur with any behavioural profile, so the pair *(clustering, originality)* is a useful "is this an echo chamber producing its own content, or echoing someone else's?" axis.

A canonical use case for Pulpit's threat-mapping mission: spotting **coordinated cells** in an otherwise open citation graph. A small group of channels that all forward each other's content has every member's local clustering near 1.0, even if the group is structurally peripheral (low betweenness, modest in-degree, low PageRank). No other measure in the suite singles them out.

**Example.** In a mapped extremist-channel ecosystem, an internal cell of five channels all cross-forwarding each other shows up with local clustering 0.6–0.8 per member, even though three of them have low PageRank and unremarkable in-degree — none of the global-prestige or brokerage measures flag them. By contrast, a mainstream news aggregator drawing from twelve mutually unrelated sources (a national newswire, an academic blog, a sports outlet, a foreign-policy magazine, …) scores near 0 on clustering even with the same edge count: its contacts don't cite each other, so no triangles pass through it. Burt's constraint reads the aggregator the same way (low constraint = open ego-network), but reverses the verdict on the cell (high constraint = redundant, embedded) — together the two measures separate `(open broker, closed echo-chamber)` cleanly along the constraint × clustering plane.

## Amplification factor

*Amplification factor scores how many times each of a channel's messages is re-broadcast, on average, by other channels in the monitored ecosystem — a per-post measure of intra-network virality.*

For a channel `v`, amplification factor is the ratio of inbound forwarding events to own message volume:

> `A(v) = R(v) / M(v)`

where `M(v)` is the count of `v`'s own messages observed in the analysis window and `R(v)` is the count of messages, in *other* in-graph channels, whose forward-origin is `v`. Numerator and denominator are both message-level counts pulled directly from the message store, not from graph edges: a single high-weight tie `u → v` still contributes one event per forward to `R(v)`, not the edge's aggregated weight, so the score reads off raw forwarding activity rather than the rescaled citation graph. A value of 1.0 means each `v` post corresponds, on average, to one re-broadcast somewhere in the network; values above 1 indicate the network forwards `v`'s output faster than `v` produces it (a high-virality regime where small but resonant sources show up most clearly); values below 1 indicate `v` mostly speaks to its own subscriber base. The score is lower-bounded at 0 and has no theoretical upper bound — a low-volume source forwarded heavily can score in the high single digits. Mathematically the quantity is the *per-seed-post influence* of Bakshy, Hofman, Mason & Watts (2011): the mean cascade size when `v` is the seed, with cascade size operationalised as total downstream forwarding events. Telegram preserves the original forward-origin through the entire forwarding chain (`fwd_from.from_id` always points to the originator, not to the most recent re-poster), so `R(v)` aggregates **cascade volume** — direct first-hop forwards and forwards-of-forwards alike — rather than only first-hop re-broadcasts.

Pulpit computes it in `apply_amplification_factor` by combining two database aggregates: `per_channel_message_counts` (the `M(v)` denominator, `Message.objects.alive()` only) and `per_channel_forwards_received` (the `R(v)` numerator, also `alive()` and restricted to `forwarded_from_id ∈ channel_pks AND channel_id ∈ channel_pks` so that *both* source and amplifier are in-graph). Both queries are time-windowed via `make_date_q(start_date, end_date)` and **period-aware** via `channel_cutoff_q()` — a message contributes only when the channel publishing it was in an in-target attribution period at the message date, the same chokepoint used by every other content metric, edge construction, and the whole-network [Amplification Ratio](whole-network-statistics.md#amplification-ratio). Channels with `M(v) = 0` receive `0.0` (matching the value the channel table renders), not `null`.

[doi:10.1145/1935826.1935845](https://doi.org/10.1145/1935826.1935845) — defines per-user influence as the average cascade size per seed post (total downstream events ÷ count of seed posts); Pulpit's amplification factor is precisely this quantity, instantiated on Telegram forwards within an analyst-defined corpus rather than on Twitter retweets at platform scale.

**Edge-weight and direction.** Amplification factor is **invariant to `--edge-weight-strategy`**: the score reads message-level forwards directly from the database, not graph edges, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` all produce identical values — unlike every other content/structural measure in the suite. The measure is **direction-aware by construction** in the sense that forwarding has an intrinsic source→amplifier direction at the message level, but does not consult the graph's edge orientation; reversing the graph would not change it. **In practice:** amplification factor is Pulpit's most direct answer to *whose voice carries the furthest, post-for-post?* It is the behavioural-side complement to the graph-position measures, and the informative cases are the disagreements:
- vs **`fans`** (subscriber count): a channel with 3 000 subscribers and amplification 4.0 reaches more channels per post than one with 50 000 subscribers and amplification 0.2 — the *million follower fallacy* of Cha et al. (2010) localised to Pulpit's ecosystem.
- vs **`INDEGCENTRALITY`** / **`HITSAUTH`**: in-degree counts *distinct* citers (it is unweighted by design) and authority weights them by hub prestige; amplification factor weights *total forwarding volume* per produced post. A channel cited only by one tireless aggregator scores low on in-degree but can score very high on amplification — the aggregator's volume is the source of the asymmetry. The pair separates *who is cited by many* (in-degree) from *whose content is forwarded heavily* (amplification).
- vs **`CONTENTORIGINALITY`**: the two read different sides of the message ledger — originality is forwards-sent ÷ own messages, amplification is forwards-received ÷ own messages — and are statistically independent. The (originality, amplification) plane gives the canonical four roles: high/high = primary source whose content travels (the ideal target for moderation-resilience analysis), high/low = original niche that nobody re-broadcasts, low/high = aggregator whose curated forwards are themselves re-amplified, low/low = passive consumer / dead branch.
- vs **`SPREADING`**: spreading efficiency simulates an SIR cascade on the *graph topology* (edge weights as transmission probabilities) — a *hypothetical* reach; amplification factor measures *observed* reach from actual forward events. When the two diverge sharply on a channel, the divergence is informative: high `SPREADING` + low amplification suggests structural potential the channel has not yet realised (or has lost — moderation, audience fatigue); low `SPREADING` + high amplification suggests an audience that re-broadcasts heavily despite a structurally peripheral position — exactly the "wide-and-shallow broadcast cascade" regime of Goel et al. (2016).
- vs **`TROPHICLEVEL`**: trophic level places `v` on the structural source→sink axis (low = original source, high = terminal amplifier) from the citation graph alone, independent of message volume; amplification factor measures the *flow* into `v` from the rest of the network per produced post. The pair complements neatly — a low trophic level + high amplification confirms a primary source whose content actually travels; low trophic level + low amplification flags a structural source whose output the network ignores.

The whole-network counterpart is the [Amplification Ratio](whole-network-statistics.md#amplification-ratio) reported in the `CONTENT` stat group, which aggregates the same `R / M` over every in-target channel and characterises the ecosystem's overall virality rate.

**Example.** A research-grade analyst's channel `s` with 3 000 subscribers publishes 200 detailed messages over the analysis window; ten mainstream aggregators in the same network forward `s` regularly, producing a combined 850 forwarding events. `A(s) = 850 / 200 = 4.25` — each `s` post is, on average, re-broadcast more than four times somewhere in the network. A party's official channel `p` with 50 000 subscribers publishes 1 200 messages in the same window; only a handful of small accounts forward it, totalling 220 events. `A(p) = 220 / 1 200 ≈ 0.18` — most `p` messages never leave their own subscriber base. Subscriber count would rank `p` an order of magnitude above `s`; amplification factor flips the ordering, correctly identifying `s` as the network's most-amplified voice on a per-post basis. The pair `(fans, amplification_factor)` is exactly the million-follower-fallacy diagnostic of Cha et al. (2010), applied to Telegram.

## Content originality

*Content originality is the share of a channel's published messages that are not Telegram forwards — a per-post measure of producer-vs-redistributor behaviour, bounded in [0, 1] and independent of network position.*

For a channel `v`, content originality is the complement of the forward-fraction of its own message stream:

> `O(v) = 1 − F(v) / M(v)`

where `M(v)` is the count of `v`'s own messages observed in the analysis window and `F(v)` is the count of those messages that carry Telegram-native forward provenance (`Message.forwarded_from is not None`). Both quantities are message-level counts pulled directly from the message store, so the score is unaffected by `--edge-weight-strategy` and by the graph's edge direction — it reads off the channel's own publishing ledger, not the citation network around it. The score is bounded in `[0, 1]`: 1.0 means every message is original, 0.0 means every message is a forward, intermediate values give the production-to-redistribution mix. Channels with `M(v) = 0` receive `null` (not 0.0, to distinguish "channel produces nothing" from "channel produces only original content"). Crucially, only **native Telegram forwards** count as `F(v)`: a message that embeds a `t.me/` link in its body is *not* counted as a forward here, even though `t.me/` references *are* used as citation edges by `graph_builder` — the asymmetry is deliberate. The forward is a discrete redistribution act with provenance preserved end-to-end by Telegram, while a `t.me/` mention is an editorial reference inside an otherwise authored post (the Telegram analogue of Twitter's retweet-vs-quote distinction in Boyd, Golder & Lotan 2010).

Pulpit computes it in `apply_content_originality` (`network/measures/_content.py`) by running `per_channel_message_counts` twice over the same time-windowed, in-target-period-filtered base query: once unfiltered for `M(v)`, once with `extra_q=Q(forwarded_from__isnull=False)` for `F(v)`. Both calls hit `Message.objects.alive()` (excluding lost messages), are time-windowed via `make_date_q(start_date, end_date)`, and are **period-aware** via `channel_cutoff_q()` — a message contributes only when its channel was in an in-target attribution period at the message date, the same chokepoint used by every other content metric, edge construction, and the whole-network [Content originality (network-level)](whole-network-statistics.md#content-originality-network-level) figure in the `CONTENT` stat group. `M(v)` is identically the `messages_count` column rendered in the channel table, so the table's Messages column, the Amplification denominator, and the Content originality denominator all reconcile by construction.

- Boyd, D., Golder, S. & Lotan, G. **Edge-weight and direction.** Content originality is **invariant to `--edge-weight-strategy`** and to graph edge direction — like Amplification factor, it consults message-level provenance directly rather than the graph, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` all produce identical values, and the score is unchanged on `G` vs `G.reverse()`. The measure is also **graph-independent**: a channel's content originality is the same whether or not its forwarding partners are present in the graph, since provenance is read off `v`'s own messages, not off in-graph edges (contrast with `AMPLIFICATION`, whose numerator only counts forwards whose source *and* amplifier are both in `channel_pks`). What *does* change the score is anything that changes which of `v`'s messages are visible to the aggregate: the analysis time window, `v`'s in-target attribution periods (out-of-period messages are excluded by `channel_cutoff_q()`), and `Message.objects.alive()` lost-message filtering.

**In practice:** Content originality is Pulpit's most direct answer to *whose voice is genuinely original, post-for-post, and whose is curatorial?* It is the behavioural-side companion of the graph-position measures, and the informative cases are the disagreements:
- vs **`AMPLIFICATION`**: forwards-sent ÷ own messages vs forwards-received ÷ own messages — the two are statistically independent and span the canonical four-role plane already detailed under [Amplification factor § In practice](#amplification-factor): high/high = primary source whose content travels; high/low = original niche nobody re-broadcasts; low/high = aggregator whose curated forwards are themselves re-amplified; low/low = passive consumer / dead branch.
- vs **`TROPHICLEVEL`**: trophic level is the *structural* counterpart — it asks whether a channel's *position in the citation flow* is upstream regardless of any single message's provenance; content originality asks whether the messages *themselves* are original. The pair separates *genuine sources* (low trophic level + high originality), *mid-chain relays* (mid trophic level + low originality), and *posers* (high originality but high trophic level — a channel whose original posts nobody downstream amplifies, sitting near the network's terminal layer).
- vs **`HITSHUB`** / **`OUTDEGCENTRALITY`**: hub score and out-degree centrality count *how many distinct channels* `v` cites or forwards from (a breadth signal on the graph side); content originality reads off *what fraction of `v`'s own stream is forwarding at all* (an intensity signal on the message side). A channel can have high hub + high originality (selective curation in otherwise original analysis) or low hub + low originality (relentless re-forwarding from one or two sources). Pair `(HITSHUB, CONTENTORIGINALITY)` to separate *editorial curators* from *single-source megaphones*.
- vs **`DIFFUSIONLAG`**: lag asks *when* `v` forwards; originality asks *how much* of `v`'s stream is forwarding at all. A high-originality channel can still have a meaningful diffusion lag on its thin forward slice.

The whole-network counterpart is the [Content originality (network-level)](whole-network-statistics.md#content-originality-network-level) figure in the `CONTENT` stat group, computed by the same formula but pooled over all in-target channels' messages — the network's overall production-to-redistribution ratio.

**Example.** Within an analyst's target ecosystem, channel `s` (a commentary outlet that publishes its own takes) emits 600 messages over the analysis window, 30 of which are native Telegram forwards from peer channels — `O(s) = 1 − 30 / 600 = 0.95`, almost every post is original with a thin layer of curated forwards. Channel `a` (a content aggregator operating on the same topics) emits 800 messages over the same window, 720 of which are forwards from a handful of sources `a` re-broadcasts — `O(a) = 1 − 720 / 800 = 0.10`, `a`'s public stream is, by volume, almost entirely other people's content. Subscriber count alone might rank `a` ahead (volume is higher), but content originality flips the framing: `s` is the network's local *producer*, `a` its local *amplifier*. Pairing with [Amplification factor](#amplification-factor) closes the analysis — if `s` also has high amplification, it is a primary source whose original content travels; if `a` does (rarer, but the network sometimes amplifies the amplifier), it is a true broadcast-style aggregator.

## Diffusion lag

*Diffusion lag is the median number of hours a channel waits before re-broadcasting content from elsewhere — a per-channel timing signature that separates early adopters from late amplifiers, independent of how often the channel forwards or how widely its own content reaches.*

For a channel `v`, diffusion lag is the median elapsed time between the original publication date of a forwarded message and the moment `v` posted the forward:

> `Δ(v) = median over f ∈ F(v) of (t_post(f) − t_origin(f))   [hours]`

where `F(v)` is the set of `v`'s forwarded messages with both endpoints of the lag available (`forwarded_from`, `fwd_from_date`, and `date` all non-null), `t_post(f)` is when `v` published the forward, and `t_origin(f)` is when the original source channel first posted the content. Telegram preserves `fwd_from.date` end-to-end on the forwarded message envelope, so every forward carries its own observed lag — the channel-level score is the empirical median across `F(v)` rather than an inferred aggregate. The **median** is used in preference to the mean because forwarding lags follow a heavy-tailed, near-power-law distribution typical of human waiting times (Barabási 2005; Iribarren & Moro 2009): a handful of anniversary or archival re-shares would dominate any mean and bury the channel's typical reaction time under tail mass. Lags are reported rounded to one decimal hour.

An optional **reaction window** `--diffusion-window DAYS` (default 30, set `0` to disable; mirrored on the Operations panel under *Linked parameters*) excludes forwards whose observed lag exceeds the window, keeping the measure focused on *contemporaneous* amplification — same news-cycle re-broadcasts — rather than retrospective re-circulation. Channels with no in-window dated forwards receive `null` (not 0, to distinguish "no forwarding to time" from "instantaneous forwarding"). Negative lags (forward stamped *before* the original — a clock-skew or out-of-order-ingest artefact) are dropped from `F(v)` rather than folded into the median.

Pulpit computes it in `apply_diffusion_lag` (`network/measures/_content.py`): the query selects forwards on `Message.objects.alive()` filtered by `forwarded_from__isnull=False & fwd_from_date__isnull=False & date__isnull=False`, time-windowed via `make_date_q(start_date, end_date)` and **period-aware** via `channel_cutoff_q()` so that out-of-period messages do not contribute (the same chokepoint used by every other content metric and by edge construction). Per-channel hours are computed in Python via `(date − fwd_from_date).total_seconds() / 3600`, screened against the reaction window, and reduced via `statistics.median`.

**References:**
- Barabási, A.-L. (2005) "The origin of bursts and heavy tails in human dynamics." *Nature* 435(7039), 207–211. [doi:10.1038/nature03459](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature03459) — establishes that human response times to communications follow heavy-tailed, bursty distributions rather than Poisson exponentials; the empirical justification for reducing per-channel lags with a robust statistic (median) rather than the mean.
- Iribarren, J.L. & Moro, E. (2009) "Impact of human activity patterns on the dynamics of information diffusion." *Physical Review Letters* 103(3), 038702. [doi:10.1103/PhysRevLett.103.038702](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevLett.103.038702) — directly applies Barabási's heavy-tailed waiting times to information diffusion, showing that propagation dynamics are governed by the *typical* (median) human response time rather than the mean. The direct precedent for Pulpit's per-channel median-of-lags formulation.
- Kwon, S., Cha, M., Jung, K., Chen, W. & Wang, Y. (2013) "Prominent features of rumor propagation in online social media." *2013 IEEE 13th International Conference on Data Mining* (ICDM). [doi:10.1109/ICDM.2013.61](https://doi.org/10.1109/ICDM.2013.61) — identifies temporal features (delays between original post and re-share, burstiness, periodicity) as one of the three dominant feature families separating rumours from non-rumours on Twitter, alongside structural and linguistic features. Diffusion lag is the channel-level analogue of their event-level lag features.
- Cheng, J., Adamic, L., Dow, P.A., Kleinberg, J. & Leskovec, J. (2014) "Can cascades be predicted?" *Proceedings of the 23rd International Conference on World Wide Web* (WWW). [doi:10.1145/2566486.2567997](https://doi.org/10.1145/2566486.2567997) — demonstrates that early-time temporal features (notably the time to the first few shares) outperform structural features when predicting cascade growth. By reading the channel's *characteristic* time-to-forward, diffusion lag identifies the actors most likely to dominate that early window.
- Vosoughi, S., Roy, D. & Aral, S. (2018) "The spread of true and false news online." *Science* 359(6380), 1146–1151. [doi:10.1126/science.aap9559](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aap9559) — large-scale evidence that false news propagates significantly *faster* than true news on Twitter (lower median time-to-`k`-retweets); diffusion lag is the per-channel projection of the same per-cascade speed metric, making it the closest Pulpit-side analogue to that paper's headline finding and the natural metric for separating threat-amplifying fast actors from slow archival accounts.

**Edge-weight and direction.** Diffusion lag is **invariant to `--edge-weight-strategy`** and to graph edge direction: like Amplification factor and Content originality, it consults message timestamps directly rather than the graph, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` all produce identical values, and the score is unchanged on `G` vs `G.reverse()`. The measure is also **graph-independent**: a channel's diffusion lag is the same whether or not its forwarding sources are present in the graph, since each lag is computed from message metadata alone (contrast with `AMPLIFICATION`, whose numerator only counts forwards whose source *and* amplifier are both in `channel_pks`). What *does* change the score is the analysis time window, the channel's in-target attribution periods (out-of-period messages are excluded by `channel_cutoff_q()`), `Message.objects.alive()` lost-message filtering, and the reaction window `--diffusion-window` itself (a wider window admits longer-tail re-shares and may shift the median *upward*; pick a setting that matches the news-cycle of interest).

**In practice:** Diffusion lag answers a question no structural measure can: *when* does a channel typically react? It is the temporal-side companion of the graph-position and behavioural-volume measures, and the informative cases are the disagreements:
- vs **`AMPLIFICATION`**: amplification measures *how much* `v`'s own content is forwarded by others; diffusion lag measures *how quickly* `v` forwards others'. The pair `(AMPLIFICATION, DIFFUSIONLAG)` separates four roles: high amplification + low lag = a fast, well-amplified channel (an *agenda-setter* in its community); high amplification + high lag = a slow but resonant channel (a *consolidator* whose late takes still travel); low amplification + low lag = a quick reactor whose forwards do not themselves spread (a *follower* with no echo); low amplification + high lag = a slow re-broadcaster on the network's periphery.
- vs **`SPREADING`**: SIR spreading efficiency simulates *hypothetical* cascade reach from a structural seed; diffusion lag reads off the *observed* speed of one specific behavioural slice (the forwarded fraction of `v`'s own stream). A channel with high `SPREADING` and low lag is structurally well-placed *and* operationally fast — the agenda-setting profile. High `SPREADING` and high lag flags a channel whose structural advantage is muted by slow reaction.
- vs **`CONTENTORIGINALITY`**: content originality measures *how much* of `v`'s stream is forwarding at all; diffusion lag measures *how fast* the forwarded slice reacts. A high-originality channel can still have a meaningful diffusion lag computed on its small forwarded fraction — informative when the rare forward is *also* slow (an editorial "deep cut" pattern) or fast (curated breaking-news amplification within an otherwise original stream).
- vs **`TROPHICLEVEL`**: trophic level places `v` on the structural source→sink axis (positional ordering on the citation graph); diffusion lag places `v` on the temporal source→sink axis (timing ordering on the forwarding chain). The two can disagree — a structural source can still be a slow forwarder of *others'* content, a downstream amplifier can react instantly. Reading them together gives the full upstream/downstream picture, structural and temporal.

Diffusion lag has no whole-network counterpart in the `CONTENT` stat group: the per-channel medians are reported individually, not pooled into a single network statistic, because the median-of-medians across heterogeneous channels would obscure the very tail structure the per-channel medians were chosen to resist.

**Example.** Within a network of nationalist commentators, two channels `a` and `b` each forward roughly 60% of a primary broadcaster's posts and have comparable in-degree. Their diffusion lags, computed with the default 30-day reaction window, are `Δ(a) = 1.8 h` and `Δ(b) = 17 h`. On every structural measure (PageRank, in-degree centrality, betweenness) and every other behavioural measure (`AMPLIFICATION`, `CONTENTORIGINALITY`) they look interchangeable; temporally they are not. `a` is part of the broadcaster's same-day distribution chain — an *agenda-setter* whose timing extends the broadcaster's acute window. `b` re-broadcasts after the news cycle has moved on — an *aggregator* whose role is consolidating narratives rather than seeding them. The same pair would be indistinguishable in any static-graph snapshot; diffusion lag is the axis that surfaces the difference.

## Spreading efficiency

*If this channel were the first to publish a piece of information, what fraction of the network would eventually receive it under an SIR cascade?*

Spreading efficiency runs a discrete-time **Susceptible–Infected–Recovered (SIR)** epidemic process (Kermack & McKendrick 1927; Hethcote 2000) seeded at each channel and reports the mean fraction of the network ever infected, in the spreader-ranking framework of Kitsak, Gallos, Havlin, Liljeros, Muchnik, Stanley & Makse (2010). For channel `v`:

> `S(v) = ⟨ |R(v) \ {v}| ⟩_runs / (n − 1)`

where `R(v)` is the set of nodes ever infected in a single run seeded at `v`, `⟨·⟩` is the Monte Carlo mean over `--spreading-runs` independent runs (default 200), and `n − 1` is the maximum reach (the whole graph minus the seed). A value of 0.32 means each cascade seeded at `v` reaches, on average, 32 % of the other channels. The seed is excluded by subtracting 1 per run before normalising, so a network of two isolated nodes scores 0.0 for both — there is no other channel to reach. Scores are rounded to six decimals.

Transmission is an independent Bernoulli draw, per infected node, per step, against each susceptible successor, with probability `p(u→w) = min(weight(u→w) / max_weight, 1.0)` — the edge weight rescaled by the maximum edge weight in the graph so the strongest tie transmits with probability 1 and the rest in proportion. The max-normalisation is load-bearing: `build_graph` already rescales raw weights to a max of 10, so a naive `min(weight, 1.0)` would saturate transmission on most edges and erase the tie-strength signal (this was a real bug, fixed in the directed-graph network-measure overhaul — see CHANGELOG). Each infected node then recovers independently per step with probability `γ = _SIR_GAMMA = 0.3` (mean infectious period ≈ 3 steps). The loop halts when no infected nodes remain. The Monte Carlo uses a deterministic seeded RNG (`np.random.default_rng(42)`) so two runs of `structural_analysis` on the same graph produce byte-identical scores.

**Direction matters.** `build_graph` writes citation edges amplifier→source (citing → cited), but information actually flows the other way — the source posts, the amplifiers re-broadcast. SIR propagates along NetworkX out-edges, so a seed at the source can only reach its amplifiers if they are reachable as out-neighbours. Pulpit therefore builds the SIR adjacency on `graph.reverse()`, matching Kitsak et al. (2010)'s diffusion direction rather than running a backward citation walk that would mean nothing. Of all the network measures, only `SPREADING` and `TROPHICLEVEL` reverse the graph; `BURTCONSTRAINT` and `LOCALCLUSTERING` symmetrise it; every other measure runs on the as-built amplifier→source orientation.

The SIR core (`sir_ever_infected` in `network/measures/_spreading.py`) is shared by construction with two other features so all three diffusion endpoints agree on the same mechanics: the **`spreading` [robustness attack](robustness-analysis.md#other-available-strategies)** removes top SIR spreaders first, the dynamic dual of the static prestige attacks; and the **`CASCADE_OVERLAP` [vacancy-analysis score](vacancy-analysis.md#score-d--cascade-overlap)** Jaccard-overlaps the vacancy's pre-closure reach set with each candidate's post-closure reach set. Both reuse `--spreading-runs`, so changing the value changes all three measures consistently.

**References:**
- Kitsak, M., Gallos, L.K., Havlin, S., Liljeros, F., Muchnik, L., Stanley, H.E. & Makse, H.A. (2010) "Identification of influential spreaders in complex networks." *Nature Physics* 6(11). [doi:10.1038/nphys1746](https://doi.org/10.1038/nphys1746) — the canonical reference for the paradigm Pulpit uses: an SIR cascade is seeded at every node in turn, the mean cascade size becomes the per-node influence score, and the ranking is treated ordinally. Also cited by Pulpit's [K-core coreness](#k-core-coreness) section for the same paper's parallel result that k-core depth predicts spreading influence better than degree does.
- Pastor-Satorras, R., Castellano, C., Van Mieghem, P. & Vespignani, A. (2015) "Epidemic processes in complex networks." *Reviews of Modern Physics* 87(3). [doi:10.1103/RevModPhys.87.925](https://doi.org/10.1103/RevModPhys.87.925) — comprehensive survey of SIR/SIS on directed weighted networks; gives the discrete-time stochastic algorithm Pulpit implements and the standard reading of the seed-reach set as a node-level influence measure.
- Newman, M.E.J. (2002) "Spread of epidemic disease on networks." *Physical Review E* 66(1), 016128. [doi:10.1103/PhysRevE.66.016128](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevE.66.016128) — foundational analytic treatment of SIR on heterogeneous networks; shows that cascade reach depends on the heterogeneity of out-degree (out-strength on a weighted graph), which is precisely the structural signal Pulpit's score reads off.
- Daley, D.J. & Kendall, D.G. (1965) "Stochastic rumours." *Journal of the Institute of Mathematics and its Applications* 1(1). [doi:10.1093/imamat/1.1.42](https://doi.org/10.1093/imamat/1.1.42) — the original adaptation of SIR-style compartmental dynamics from disease to *rumour* propagation: the conceptual bridge from epidemiology to information diffusion that legitimises running an SIR cascade on a citation graph at all.
- Kermack, W.O. & McKendrick, A.G. (1927) "A contribution to the mathematical theory of epidemics." *Proceedings of the Royal Society A* 115(772). [doi:10.1098/rspa.1927.0118](https://doi.org/10.1098/rspa.1927.0118) — the foundational SIR formulation. Also cited by Pulpit's [`CASCADE_OVERLAP` vacancy-analysis score](vacancy-analysis.md#score-d--cascade-overlap), so the two SIR-based features share the same epidemiological anchor.

**Edge-weight choice.** Spreading-efficiency rankings depend on `--edge-weight-strategy` through the per-edge transmission probability `weight / max_weight`: `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` (every edge worth 1, so the max-normalisation is a no-op and every infected node transmits to every successor with certainty along every tie — the most aggressive cascade regime) produce materially different orderings. The choice is the same one that affects HITS, betweenness, harmonic, Burt's constraint and the trophic level. See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies).

**Caveat: read ordinally, not as a calibrated probability.** Turning a citation frequency into an infection probability has no canonical recipe; the max-normalisation is a heuristic chosen so the strongest tie in the graph behaves as the certain-transmission ceiling and the rest scale linearly below it. Alternative mappings (`1 − exp(−β · weight)`, row-stochastic normalisation, …) shift absolute values but largely preserve the ranking. Tying the single hottest edge to probability 1 is itself a modelling choice, and changing `--spreading-runs` or `γ` shifts the absolute scale. Pulpit therefore reports the score as an **ordinal ranking aid** — `S(A) > S(B)` is the load-bearing reading, the precise gap between them is not.

**In practice:** spreading efficiency is Pulpit's only purely *dynamic* per-node reach measure — every other measure characterises a channel by its static position. It complements four families of measures and the informative cases are the disagreements:
- vs **`PAGERANK`** and **`HITSAUTH`**: stationary-distribution / eigenvector prestige measures assume an indefinite random walk; SIR simulates a finite, decaying cascade with a recovery rule. A channel can score high on PageRank yet poorly on `SPREADING` when its high-prestige neighbours hold each other in tight reciprocal loops that recover before the cascade leaves them — the eigenvector mass that PageRank rewards does not always translate to onward propagation.
- vs **`CORENESS`**: the headline finding of Kitsak et al. (2010) was that **k-core depth predicts SIR spreading efficiency better than degree** does. The two measures should therefore correlate, and they do in well-formed networks; the informative cases are the rare disagreements (low-coreness high-`SPREADING` channels — peripheral seeds whose few out-edges happen to land on a high-coverage region; high-coreness low-`SPREADING` channels — recovery-dominated, locally dense neighbourhoods that trap the cascade).
- vs **`AMPLIFICATION`**: `SPREADING` simulates a *hypothetical* cascade on graph topology; amplification factor measures *observed* per-post re-broadcasting. The pair separates structural potential from realised reach — high `SPREADING` + low amplification = structural potential the channel has not realised (or has lost — moderation, audience fatigue); low `SPREADING` + high amplification = observed reach the topology alone does not predict (often the wide-and-shallow broadcast cascade regime of Goel et al. 2016).
- vs **`BRIDGINGCENTRALITY`** / **`BETWEENNESS`**: brokerage measures reward sitting *between* dense regions; spreading efficiency rewards sitting *above* one. A bridge between two clusters can score lower on `SPREADING` than a node inside one of those clusters with denser local reach — the SIR cascade tends to die in the bottleneck before percolating into the second cluster.
- vs **`TROPHICLEVEL`**: trophic level places `v` on the structural source→sink axis but says nothing about how *efficient* `v` is as a source. Low trophic + low `SPREADING` = a structural source whose content does not actually propagate; low trophic + high `SPREADING` = the canonical "key influencer" the SIR ranking is designed to find.

**Example.** In a mapped political ecosystem of ~400 channels, a small commentary outlet `s` with 4 000 subscribers has a second-decile PageRank and an unremarkable in-degree of 7 — every static prestige column dismisses it. Its `SPREADING` score, however, is 0.32: each SIR cascade seeded at `s` reaches, on average, a third of the rest of the network. Inspection reveals `s` sits one hop upstream of three of the ecosystem's largest aggregators, all of which forward `s` heavily and themselves fan out to dozens of dependent channels; the cascade percolates through that two-step neighbourhood quickly enough to outrun recovery, so the mean infected fraction is high even though direct citers are few. A second channel `p` — the official channel of a political party — has the network's highest in-degree (78) and a top-quintile PageRank, yet its `SPREADING` score is 0.11: the channels that cite it are mostly peripheral leaves with sparse onward connectivity, and the cascade dies within one hop. Static prestige ranks `p` first and misses `s` entirely; spreading efficiency reverses the ordering, correctly identifying `s` as the network's *dynamical* superspreader in the Kitsak et al. (2010) sense — the channel whose voice, post for post, would percolate furthest if the network were to relay a single piece of content.

## Bridging centrality (Hwang et al. 2008)

*A high bridging centrality score means this channel is a topological bridge — a low-degree node wedged between high-degree regions, whose removal would most fragment the network.*

Bridging centrality (Hwang, Kim, Ramanathan & Zhang 2008) is the product of betweenness centrality and a **bridging coefficient** that captures the *position* of a node in the degree landscape rather than its global flow share. For each node `v`:

> `C_bridge(v) = C_B(v) · Ψ(v)`,    with    `Ψ(v) = (1 / d(v)) / Σ_{i ∈ N(v)} (1 / d(i))`

where `d(v)` is the **undirected, unweighted** degree of `v` (the count of distinct neighbours — predecessors ∪ successors of the directed graph, minus self) and `N(v)` is the neighbour set. The bridging coefficient is large when a channel has *few* links of its own yet those links reach *high-degree* nodes — the small `1/d(i)` terms shrink the denominator — so it grows on narrow waists between otherwise busy regions. Multiplied by betweenness, it keeps only those waists that actually carry shortest-path traffic: leaves get a high bridging coefficient but `C_B = 0` and so a final score of 0, and central aggregators *inside* a dense cluster get a low coefficient and so a low final score even when their betweenness is high. The bridging coefficient is unbounded (it can exceed 1 when a low-degree node points only to high-degree neighbours), so the product is not normalised to `[0, 1]` — the ranking is the load-bearing reading, not the absolute scale.

Pulpit's bridging coefficient follows Hwang's original definition exactly: `compute_bridging_coefficient` (`network/measures/_centrality.py`) takes the *unweighted, undirected* degree (`|predecessors ∪ successors − {v}|`, isolated nodes get `0.0`) and computes Ψ over the same neighbour set. Direction is dropped on purpose — the coefficient is a topological-position quantity, so a citation either way counts the two endpoints as neighbours. The **betweenness factor**, by contrast, is computed via Pulpit's project-wide `compute_betweenness`: NetworkX's directed `betweenness_centrality` over the `distance = 1 / weight` proximity projection of Opsahl, Agneessens & Skvoretz (2010). This is a deliberate adaptation — Hwang assumes unweighted, undirected betweenness on an undirected graph — and it keeps the bridging-centrality factor on the same scale as the standalone [Betweenness centrality](#betweenness-centrality) column. The orchestrator pre-computes a single betweenness once and shares it across the standalone column, Hwang bridging centrality and [community bridging](#community-bridging), so the three measures cannot drift.

Crucially this is **purely topological** — it uses no community partition, so it needs no `--community-strategies` basis. It answers a different question from [community bridging](#community-bridging): "is this a bridge between *dense regions of the graph*?" rather than "is this a broker between *detected communities*?".

**Reference:** Hwang, W., Kim, T., Ramanathan, M. & Zhang, A. (2008) "Bridging Centrality: Graph Mining from Element Level to Group Level." *Proceedings of the 14th ACM SIGKDD International Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining* (KDD '08). [doi:10.1145/1401890.1401941](https://doi.org/10.1145/1401890.1401941) — the canonical reference; introduces the bridging coefficient `(1/d(v)) / Σ 1/d(i)`, argues that the betweenness × bridging-coefficient product specifically targets topological bridges (nodes whose removal maximally fragments the giant component), and benchmarks the measure on protein–protein interaction and AS-level Internet graphs against degree-, betweenness- and information-centrality baselines.

**Edge-weight choice.** Mixed. The bridging coefficient `Ψ(v)` is computed on unweighted, undirected degrees and is therefore **invariant to `--edge-weight-strategy`**: `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES`, `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` and `NONE` all produce identical Ψ values. The betweenness factor *does* depend on the strategy, however: Pulpit feeds the `1 / weight` proximity projection into `nx.betweenness_centrality`, so changing the strategy reshuffles betweenness and therefore the final bridging-centrality ranking. The two factors composing the score thus respond differently to the configuration — worth knowing when reading the column alongside the standalone betweenness. See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies).

**In practice:** bridging centrality separates *true bottlenecks* from *mere hubs*. A hub with many neighbours can have very high betweenness yet a low bridging coefficient (most of its neighbours are themselves high-degree cluster members whose `1/d(i)` terms swell the denominator), so it scores *lower* than a modest channel that quietly connects two large clusters — the one whose disappearance fractures the network. The informative cases are the disagreements with the related measures Pulpit reports:

- vs **`BETWEENNESS`**: betweenness alone surfaces every globally-trafficked node, hubs included; bridging centrality discards the hubs whose load lies *inside* one dense region. A channel that ranks high on betweenness *and* on bridging centrality is a genuine inter-region bottleneck; one that ranks high on betweenness *but low* on bridging centrality is a within-region aggregator whose removal would re-route traffic but not split the network. This is the pairing the [brokerage robustness attack](robustness-analysis.md#brokerage-attack-betweenness) implicitly relies on — the attack ranks by plain betweenness, and bridging centrality lets the analyst read off, *before running the attack*, which of those top-betweenness channels are the genuine bridges.
- vs **`BURTCONSTRAINT`** (Burt 1992 structural holes): Hwang's measure is a *degree-based topological-bridge index*; Burt's is an *interaction-redundancy index* on the ego-network. Both should agree on the canonical low-degree-broker case; disagreement points to brokers whose ego-network has no triangles (low Burt) but whose few contacts are not themselves high-degree (low Hwang) — *local* brokers between small groups rather than *network-wide* topological bridges. The two together cleanly split brokers into local-and-broad-impact, local-only, network-wide-and-broad-impact, and network-wide-only quadrants.
- vs **`BRIDGING`** (community bridging): both fold a topology-quality factor into betweenness, but Hwang's factor is purely degree-based (no partition needed), while community bridging uses the neighbour-community participation coefficient (Guimerà & Amaral 2005) and needs a [community partition](community-detection.md). Hwang asks "wedge between *dense regions*?"; community bridging asks "broker between *detected communities*?". The two often agree but can disagree when the detected community structure is coarser or finer than the degree landscape — e.g. a single detected community that contains two degree-distinct sub-clusters will show Hwang flagging the inter-sub-cluster bridge while community bridging dismisses it (both endpoints sit in the same community).

**Example.** A mapped political ecosystem of ~400 channels contains a 200-channel nationalist cluster and a 200-channel religious-conservative cluster that otherwise share no direct contact. Channel `a` is an aggregator *inside* the nationalist cluster: in-degree 47, PageRank top-decile, the highest plain betweenness in the network — a structurally well-placed within-cluster hub, and the channel every prestige column picks first. Channel `b` has only two meaningful ties: one into each of the two clusters. `b`'s in-degree is 2, its PageRank middling, and its plain betweenness three times smaller than `a`'s — every static prestige column dismisses it. The bridging coefficients invert the ordering. `a`'s many neighbours are themselves cluster members of mid-to-high degree, so the `Σ 1/d(i)` denominator is large and `Ψ(a)` is small; `b`'s two neighbours are both high-degree cluster hubs — taking both at degree ≈ 200, `Ψ(b) = (1/2) / (1/200 + 1/200) = 50`. Multiplied through, bridging centrality flags `b` first — correctly identifying the single point of failure between the two clusters, the one whose silencing would split the coalition — where plain betweenness ranks `a` first and misses the bridge entirely.

## Community bridging

*A high community bridging score means this channel is both structurally central AND bridges genuinely distinct communities.*

Community bridging is a **project composite**: the product of betweenness centrality (Freeman 1977) and the participation coefficient (Guimerà & Amaral 2005) of the node's weighted-neighbour community distribution. For each node `v`:

> `C_comm-bridge(v) = C_B(v) · P(v)`,    with    `P(v) = 1 − Σ_c (w_c / W)²`

where `c` ranges over communities, `w_c` is the total edge weight from `v` to community `c`, and `W = Σ_c w_c` is the total weighted neighbour mass. `P(v)` is 0 when every neighbour belongs to a single community (no bridging) and approaches `1 − 1/N_M` as `v`'s ties spread evenly across `N_M` modules. Because `P ∈ [0, 1]`, the product stays on betweenness' own scale — the channel scores high only when it is *simultaneously* on structurally important paths *and* those paths cross community boundaries. Pulpit is explicit that the *product itself* is not a single named published index: both factors are canonical (Freeman 1977 betweenness; Guimerà & Amaral 2005 participation), but their multiplication is a project composite — read it as *"central **and** spanning"*, not as a textbook centrality.

Two implementation details adapt the original definitions to a directed, weighted citation graph:
- **Weighting.** Guimerà & Amaral (2005) defined the participation coefficient on *unweighted* metabolic networks (`w_c` = count of links to community `c`). Pulpit uses the **weighted generalisation** that has since become standard in the complex-networks literature (Rubinov & Sporns 2010): `w_c` aggregates *edge weights* rather than link counts, so heavily-forwarded ties get more say. The formula is unchanged — only the inputs are.
- **Direction.** The graph is directed (amplifier→source), but the participation coefficient sums weight contributions from *both* predecessors and successors per neighbour-community. A v→u edge contributes `weight(v→u)` to `community(u)`; a u→v edge contributes `weight(u→v)` to `community(u)`. The score therefore reflects *all* of `v`'s community-tagged contacts, not just outgoing-citation targets. Nodes with no community-tagged neighbours, or all neighbours in one community, receive `P = 0`. The betweenness factor, by contrast, runs on Pulpit's directed weighted betweenness (`distance = 1 / weight`, Opsahl-Agneessens-Skvoretz 2010), so the citation direction *does* matter on that side.

> **Naming note.** This composite (betweenness × community participation coefficient) was previously labelled "Bridging centrality", but that name properly belongs to the degree-based measure of Hwang et al. (2008) described above — now exposed separately as `BRIDGINGCENTRALITY`. This measure was therefore renamed **Community bridging** (node key `community_bridging`); the question it answers ("does this broker span *distinct communities*?") is the Guimerà–Amaral one. Pulpit also previously used the **Shannon entropy** of the neighbour-community distribution as the second factor — bounded only by `log N_M`, so the product had no fixed ceiling. The switch to the participation coefficient keeps the second factor in `[0, 1]` and matches the canonical community-role literature.

The community basis for the participation coefficient is set by the strategy name in parentheses — for example, `BRIDGING(LOUVAIN)` uses the Louvain partition. Without a strategy name, `LEIDEN_DIRECTED` is used (the directed null model respects citation direction, which matches the brokerage question). The chosen strategy must also appear in `--community-strategies`, otherwise `structural_analysis` aborts with a configuration error. In the Operations panel the basis is picked from the **Bridging basis** dropdown in the *Linked parameters* fieldset; the same dropdown also drives the **community-bridging robustness attack** (`bridging` / `bridging(<strategy>)`) — both call the same `compute_neighbour_community_participation` helper on the same partition, so the per-node ranking the measure reports and the removal order the attack uses are byte-identical for the same configuration.

**References:**
- Guimerà, R. & Amaral, L.A.N. (2005) "Functional cartography of complex metabolic networks." *Nature* 433(7028). [doi:10.1038/nature03288](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature03288) — defines the participation coefficient `P = 1 − Σ (k_is/k_i)²` as the canonical community-role quantity, alongside the within-module degree z-score; Pulpit reuses this same helper for the [Within-module role](#within-module-role) measure (`MODULEROLE`), so the two readings of "community role" share one quantity by construction.
- Freeman, L.C. (1977) "A set of measures of centrality based on betweenness." *Sociometry* 40(1). [doi:10.2307/3033543](https://doi.org/10.2307/3033543) — the betweenness factor of the composite, already cited in full under [Betweenness centrality](#betweenness-centrality); the shared betweenness computation makes the standalone column, the Hwang [Bridging centrality](#bridging-centrality-hwang-et-al-2008) and Community bridging three readings of the same Brandes (2001) pass.
- Rubinov, M. & Sporns, O. (2010) "Complex network measures of brain connectivity: Uses and interpretations." *NeuroImage* 52(3). [doi:10.1016/j.neuroimage.2009.10.003](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuroimage.2009.10.003) — the canonical reference for the **weighted** generalisation of Guimerà & Amaral's participation coefficient (`w_c` = community-summed edge weight rather than link count), and for treating the participation coefficient as a *connector* / *cross-community broker* score in directed, weighted complex networks beyond the original metabolic-network setting.

**Edge-weight choice.** Both factors of the composite depend on `--edge-weight-strategy`. The participation coefficient sums edge weights into the `w_c` bins, so `TOTAL`, `PARTIAL_MESSAGES` and `PARTIAL_REFERENCES` reshuffle the bins in different ways, and `NONE` collapses them to plain neighbour-community counts (the original Guimerà-Amaral 2005 form). The betweenness factor consumes the `1 / weight` distance projection, so the same strategy choice reshuffles betweenness too. See [Edge-weight strategies](configuration.md#edge-weight-strategies).

**In practice:** community bridging fills a gap left by betweenness alone — a channel can rank highly on betweenness simply because it sits in a densely connected region, even if all its neighbours belong to the same ideological cluster. Community bridging penalises that: the participation factor discounts intra-community connectors and elevates genuine cross-community bridges. The informative cases are the disagreements with the related measures Pulpit reports:

- vs **`BETWEENNESS`**: every high–community-bridging channel must have non-trivial betweenness (the product would be 0 otherwise), but the reverse is not true. A node with the network's top betweenness but with all neighbours in one community gets `P = 0` and so community bridging 0 — a within-community kingpin, not a cross-community broker. The pair `(BETWEENNESS, BRIDGING)` cleanly splits brokers into *within-community kingpins* (high betweenness, low community bridging), *genuine cross-community brokers* (high on both), and *peripheral spanners* (low on both).
- vs **`BRIDGINGCENTRALITY`** (Hwang et al. 2008): both fold a topology-quality factor into betweenness, but Hwang's factor is purely degree-based (no partition), while community bridging needs a [community partition](community-detection.md). Hwang asks "wedge between *dense regions*?"; community bridging asks "broker between *detected communities*?". Disagreement points to bridges that span communities the degree landscape doesn't separate (high community bridging, low Hwang) or wedge between high-degree regions inside one community (high Hwang, low community bridging).
- vs **`MODULEROLE`** (Guimerà–Amaral within-module role): the within-module role uses the *same* participation-coefficient helper but pairs it with the within-module degree z-score (not betweenness), producing a categorical role label (connector, connector-hub, kinless, …). Reading the two together lets you separate *connector hubs* (high `MODULEROLE` participation + high within-module z + high betweenness) from *bridge connectors* with no within-community prominence (high community bridging + low within-module z) — both are cross-community brokers, but with different removal consequences.
- vs the [**community-bridging robustness attack**](robustness-analysis.md#other-available-strategies): the attack ranks channels by the same score and removes them in that order — community bridging is the *prediction*, the attack is the *measurement* of fragmentation that follows. Agreement validates the measure; disagreement points to second-order effects the static composite cannot anticipate (e.g. removals that cascade by re-exposing further structural holes).

**Example.** Two channels have nearly identical betweenness scores. Channel `a` sits at the centre of a densely-cross-citing nationalist bloc: of its weighted neighbour mass `W = 100`, all 100 units land on community `X` — so `P(a) = 1 − (100/100)² = 0`, and community bridging is `0`. Channel `b` has the same betweenness but its weighted neighbour mass splits across four distinct communities — nationalist, religious-conservative, mainstream right, and state media — with weight bins `[30, 30, 25, 15]` and `W = 100`. Its participation coefficient is `P(b) = 1 − ((30/100)² + (30/100)² + (25/100)² + (15/100)²) = 1 − (0.09 + 0.09 + 0.0625 + 0.0225) = 1 − 0.265 = 0.735`. Multiplied through, community bridging puts `b` at `0.735 × betweenness(b)` and `a` at exactly `0` — even though plain betweenness ranks them tied. Hwang's `BRIDGINGCENTRALITY` would also rank `b` higher than `a` *if and only if* the four community-hubs `b` connects happen to be high-degree nodes; if instead `b`'s four neighbours are themselves low-degree, only community bridging (which knows about the partition) flags `b` as the strategically significant cross-community node.

## K-core coreness

*A high coreness number means this channel sits in the densely interconnected nucleus of the network; a low number means it is a peripheral amplifier shed in the first peeling rounds.*

The k-core decomposition repeatedly strips away every node with fewer than *k* neighbours until only nodes with at least *k* surviving neighbours remain; a channel's **coreness** is the largest *k* whose core still contains it. It is computed on the symmetrised, self-loop-free citation graph (matching the [K-core community strategy](community-detection.md#k-core)), so it measures topological depth rather than tie strength. Coreness is one of the most robust predictors of spreading influence: Kitsak et al. (2010) showed the most effective spreaders are the channels in the network's core, often outranking both degree and betweenness, and the measure stays well-behaved on the sparse, partially disconnected graphs typical of Telegram ecosystems.

**Reference:** Kitsak, M., Gallos, L.K., Havlin, S., Liljeros, F., Muchnik, L., Stanley, H.E. & Makse, H.A. (2010) "Identification of influential spreaders in complex networks." *Nature Physics* 6(11). [doi:10.1038/nphys1746](https://doi.org/10.1038/nphys1746)

**In practice:** a channel with high in-degree but low coreness is cited by many peripheral accounts yet sits outside the mutually-reinforcing nucleus — its reach is shallower than its raw citation count suggests. A channel with modest degree but high coreness is wired into the dense core, a position that predicts efficient onward spread. Pair coreness with PageRank to separate "popular but peripheral" from "core and influential".

## Trophic level

*A low trophic level means this channel is a structural source that others draw from; a high level means it is a terminal amplifier downstream of the content it carries.*

Trophic level places every channel on a single source→sink axis derived purely from the direction of citation flow. Pulpit uses the **hierarchical levels** of MacKay, Johnson & Sansom (2020): the levels `h` solve the Laplacian system `(diag(u) − (W + Wᵀ)) h = (w_in − w_out)`, where `w_in` / `w_out` are weighted in-/out-strength and `u = w_in + w_out`. Unlike the classic Levine (1980) trophic level — undefined on graphs without basal nodes — this formulation stays finite on the cyclic citation graphs Pulpit builds. Levels are shifted so the lowest channel in each weakly-connected component reads 0.

**Reference:** MacKay, R.S., Johnson, S. & Sansom, B. (2020) "How directed is a directed network?" *Royal Society Open Science* 7(9). [doi:10.1098/rsos.201138](https://doi.org/10.1098/rsos.201138). Levine, S. (1980) "Several measures of trophic structure applicable to complex food webs." *Journal of Theoretical Biology* 83(2). [doi:10.1016/0022-5193(80)90288-X](https://doi.org/10.1016/0022-5193(80)90288-X)

**In practice:** trophic level is the structural counterpart of [content originality](#content-originality). Content originality asks whether a channel's *messages* are original; trophic level asks whether its *position in the citation flow* is upstream — regardless of how original any single post is. A channel can repost heavily (low content originality) yet still sit upstream of an even more derivative downstream cluster. Reading the two together separates genuine sources from mid-chain relays.

## Within-module role

*Is this channel a hub inside its own community, a bridge between communities, or a peripheral member? The within-module role names the position directly.*

Following Guimerà & Amaral (2005), each channel is characterised by two quantities measured against the community partition (the [bridging basis](#community-bridging), defaulting to Leiden Directed): the **within-module degree z-score** `z` — how many more intra-community neighbours it has than its community's average — and the **participation coefficient** `P` — how evenly its ties spread across communities. The (z, P) pair maps to one of seven canonical roles: ultra-peripheral, peripheral, connector, and kinless non-hubs (`z < 2.5`); and provincial, connector, and kinless hubs (`z ≥ 2.5`). The numeric `z` is the sortable **Within-module z** column; the categorical label is the **Role** column.

**Reference:** Guimerà, R. & Amaral, L.A.N. (2005) "Functional cartography of complex metabolic networks." *Nature* 433(7028). [doi:10.1038/nature03288](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature03288)

**In practice:** the role taxonomy turns a community partition into a per-channel job description. A *provincial hub* is a kingpin within a single community — central but inward-facing. A *connector hub* is both central and spans communities: the most strategically significant brokers, whose removal both fragments a community and severs cross-community flow. A (non-hub) *connector* is a low-profile bridge. `MODULEROLE` needs at least one `--community-strategies` partition to define the modules.
